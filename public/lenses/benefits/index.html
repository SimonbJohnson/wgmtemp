How far removed might people feel they are from the benefits of science?

The Lens of Benefits

Highlighting areas in Kenya, Malawi and South Africa which perceive themselves to be excluded from the positive activities and effects of science.

In 1991, Raza et.al proposed that there is a natural cultural distance between science and the people’s cultural thought complex. This cultural distance is determined by regional differences, economic conditions, educational attainment, access to media, age, cultural and religious predispositions1. We made use of the theoretical considerations of cultural distance, analysing the questions asked in the Wellcome Global Monitor to identify countries and population segments which perceive themselves to be largely removed or most distant from the benefits of science.

We found regional differences within South Africa, Kenya and Malawi characterized by resource deprivation and poverty to be the greater predictor of cultural distance to science as compared to living in a rural or urban location. Educational attainment is the biggest predictor of cultural distance to science.

Distrust is correlated with feeling excluded from the benefits of science.

Globally, distrust in science is moderately correlated (0.65 correlation) with saying science does not benefit their country.
